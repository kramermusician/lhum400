"If I know a few numbers, what can I reasonably expect to happen?"

That is exactly what a spreadsheet is built to answer.

Definition

A spreadsheet is a grid
that does math for you.

Every box can hold a number, a word, or a calculation that updates automatically when the numbers change.

Three names, one tool

They all work the same way.

Google Sheets
free, in your browser
Microsoft Excel
the industry standard
Apple Numbers
free on Mac and iPhone

Learn one and you have essentially learned all three. Same grid, same formulas.

The basic unit

A cell is one box.

ABC
1B2
2B2

Every cell has an address: a column letter and a row number. This one is B2.

Getting oriented

Rows go across.
Columns go down.

ABC
1gigpaytips

Rows are numbered 1, 2, 3. Columns are lettered A, B, C.

Every formula starts with =

A few formulas worth knowing.

=B2+B3  adds two cells
=SUM(B2:B10)  adds a whole range
=AVERAGE(B2:B10)  the typical value
=B2*4  multiply (e.g. 4 weekends)

The = sign tells the sheet to calculate instead of just displaying text.

Let the numbers signal for you

Conditional formatting

You set a rule: color a cell green when you made money, red when you lost it. The sheet applies it automatically.

+$60 +$45 $0 -$30 +$120

You see the problem cell at a glance, without reading every number.

Why this matters

Track what happened.
Forecast what is next.

Rent, gear, taxes, a tour, a savings goal: same grid, same formulas. A spreadsheet lets you test a decision before it costs you anything.
