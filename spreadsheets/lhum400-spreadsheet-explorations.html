Basics · 8:28

Google Sheets, the whole tour.

How to Digital · May 2026

One pass over everything: the grid, cell addresses, formatting, formulas, charts, sorting, and sharing. It builds a small expense tracker along the way, which is the same shape as our budget scenario.

  • Notice how every skill circles back to the cell address. B3 is column B, row 3; that is the whole coordinate system.
  • When the formula bar appears, watch where the formula lives versus where the answer shows.
Try it after: rebuild the expense tracker from the video with your own last five real purchases, then add a SUM. Ten minutes, and the skill is yours instead of the video's.
Advanced pick 1 · 9:00

Pivot tables: ask a big pile of data a question.

Champion Challander Tech · July 2025

Our walkthrough works on grids with ten rows. Real data has a thousand. A pivot table is how you ask "total by month? by category? by client?" without writing a single formula, and this video goes from zero to a working pivot on a 100-row table.

  • The why comes first: summarize fast, spot patterns. Same idea as this course, put light on the question.
  • Catch the data-range trick: leaving the range open-ended so the pivot updates itself when new rows arrive.
Try it after: in the Measure of a Plan tracker from our example sheets, find the transactions tab and build one pivot: spending by category. Compare it with the dashboard's own chart; you just rebuilt part of it yourself.
Advanced pick 2 · 7:36

VLOOKUP and XLOOKUP: teach sheets to find things.

Jotform · June 2026

A lookup pulls information from one table into another: type a product name, get its price; type a student, get their section. This one shows the classic VLOOKUP, then the modern XLOOKUP, in Excel and in Google Sheets, which is a nice proof of our line that they are one grid in different clothes.

  • Watch what breaks VLOOKUP (it can only look to the right) and how XLOOKUP shrugs that off.
  • Every lookup has the same three parts: what you are looking for, where to look, what to bring back.
Try it after: make a tiny two-column price list (five items, five prices), then in another corner of the sheet write one XLOOKUP that fetches a price by name. Change the price; watch the lookup follow.
