LEVEL 0

What is this thing?

A spreadsheet is a grid of boxes. Each box can hold a word or a number. And any box can do math using the other boxes.

People kept grids like this on paper for centuries: one row per thing, one column per fact about it. Rent here, groceries there, total at the bottom. The digital version keeps the same shape and adds one superpower: when a number changes, every total that depends on it updates itself. Instantly. Without being asked.

If you have never opened a spreadsheet in your life, you are in exactly the right place. This page assumes nothing.

LEVEL 0

Why bother?

Your head can hold maybe four numbers before they start trading places. A grid holds all of them, in daylight, at once.

But the math is not really the point. The point is the question underneath it. Can I afford this move? Which gig is actually worth my Saturday? Where does my money go every month? Questions like these feel foggy when they live in your head. Put the numbers in a grid and the fog lifts: the question becomes something you can look at, poke, and answer.

The working idea for this whole page: a spreadsheet puts light on a question, so the action you take next is a smarter one. Data first, decision second.
HOW THIS WORKS

The grids below are real.

  • Click a box to select it. Its contents show in the bar above the grid.
  • Type in the bar and press Enter. Or double-click a box to type inside it.
  • Nothing can break. Press Reset any time.
STEP 1

Every box has an address.

Columns are letters, rows are numbers. A box's address is column + row, like B2. Think of a seat at a show: section B, row 2. The address is how you find one box among many, and later it is how formulas find each other.

Click the box in column C, row 3. The little bar on the left should read C3.

STEP 2

Type your first number.

  1. Click B1.
  2. Type 42 and press Enter.

Two things happened. The number got saved into the box, and the selection dropped down one row, ready for the next entry. That little hop is why data entry in a spreadsheet goes fast.

Now click A1 and type Coffee. Notice the grid treats it differently: words sit on the left, numbers sit on the right. The grid already knows which is which.

Made a mess? Click any box and press Delete to clear it, or just retype. Nothing here is precious.

STEP 3

A box does one of three jobs.

LabelWords that name things: "Rent", "Total". No math.
InputA number you type and can change.
FormulaA calculation that starts with =.

Column A is all labels. B1 and B2 are inputs. B3 is a formula. The colors match the jobs.

Change B1 and watch B3 update itself. You never touched B3.

STEP 4

Formulas start with =

The = tells the box to calculate instead of just holding what you typed. Here is what happens, in slow motion, when you press Enter on =B1+B2:

  1. The box reads the recipe: "go get B1, go get B2, add them."
  2. It fetches those values and does the math.
  3. It shows the result, and it remembers the recipe.

That last part matters. The box does not store the answer. It stores the recipe, and re-cooks it any time an ingredient changes.

  • =B1+B2 adds · =B1-B2 subtracts
  • =B1*3 multiplies · =B1/2 divides

Click B3, type =B1+B2, press Enter. It should turn blue and show 13.

Point, don't type. =8+5 also gives 13, but it is a dead end: change your numbers and it sits there, wrong. =B1+B2 points at the boxes, so it stays true no matter what you put in them. Pointing is the whole trick.
STEP 5

Add a whole column: the range.

A range is a run of boxes: B1:B3 means B1 through B3. Feed it to SUM.

Click C1, type =SUM(B1:B3), press Enter. You should get 60. (Tip: click-drag down column B to see a range in the bar.)

STEP 6

Ask a column some questions.

SUM has cousins, and each one answers a different question about the same pile of numbers:

  • =AVERAGE(B1:B5) · what's typical?
  • =MIN(B1:B5) · what's the worst day?
  • =MAX(B1:B5) · what's the best?
  • =COUNT(B1:B5) · how many entries are there?

Below: minutes of practice, Monday through Friday. In C1, type =AVERAGE(B1:B5). You should get 24.

Now change Wednesday's 0 to 60 and watch the average jump. One unusual day can drag a whole average around. That is worth knowing before you trust any average, including the ones in headlines.

STEP 7

Teach a box to decide: IF.

IF asks a yes-or-no question and shows one answer or the other:

=IF(test, answer if yes, answer if no)

The test uses comparison signs: > more than, < less than, >= at least, <= at most. Words in a formula go inside straight double quotes, like "OK".

B1 holds what's left of a budget. Click C1 and type =IF(B1>=0, "OK", "OVER"). Then change B1 to -40 and watch the verdict flip.

A box that watches a number and raises a flag when it crosses a line: that is the seed of every dashboard you have ever seen.

Now, the ladder.

Everything below is a real question someone in this room could be asking this semester. Each one starts foggy and ends as a number you can act on. Climb until one of them bites.

SCENARIO 1

What can the band afford?

The pizza question.

Labels are done. Fill the inputs, write two formulas.

  1. Inputs: B1 players (4), B2 hours (1), B3 rate (15).
  2. B4: =B1*B2*B3 (total earned).
  3. B5: =B4/18 (pizzas at $18).
Target: $60 earned, about 3.33 pizzas.

Now change Hours to 3. Both answers jump, and nobody redid any math. That is the machine working for you.

SCENARIO 2

Where does the month actually go?

A one-month budget.

Money in, money out, what's left.

  1. B4 ("Spent"): =SUM(B2:B3).
  2. B5 ("Left over"): =B1-B4.
Target: Spent 950, Left over 250.

"Left over" reads from "Spent," which reads from the expenses. Change Rent and the change flows all the way down. Chains like this are how big models are built: each box asks the one before it.

SCENARIO 3

When can I actually have the gear?

Forecast a savings goal.

You want $600 for gear, saving $40 a week.

  1. B3 ("Saved so far"): =B1*B2.
  2. B5 ("Still need"): =B4-B3.
Target: Saved 480, Still need 120.

Change Weeks until "Still need" hits 0. You just ran a forecast: same grid, pointed at the future instead of the past.

SCENARIO 4

What do the little monthly charges really cost?

The subscription audit.

Small monthly numbers hide their real size. Multiply by 12 and they come out of hiding.

  1. B4 ("Per month"): =SUM(B1:B3).
  2. B5 ("Per year"): =B4*12.
Target: 25 per month, 300 per year.

300 dollars is a decent microphone. Delete one row's number and watch the year shrink. If you just felt a little tug about which one to cut, notice what happened: the data asked you a question you were not asking yourself.

SCENARIO 5

Which gig actually pays better?

Compare two options side by side.

Gig A pays $120 for a 3-hour night, door to door. Gig B pays $200 but eats 6 hours with the drive. Put each option in its own column and ask the same question of both.

  1. B3 (Gig A per hour): =B1/B2.
  2. C3 (Gig B per hour): =C1/C2.
Target: Gig A 40 per hour, Gig B about 33.33.

The bigger check lost. The flat fee was hiding the drive, and the per-hour number put light on it. This is the pattern for comparing anything: apartments, laptops, tour routings. One column per option, one row per fact, one formula asked of every column.

SCENARIO 6

How many tickets until the show pays for itself?

Break-even, with a verdict.

You are thinking about renting a room for a show. The room holds 40 people. Three formulas take you from costs to a straight answer.

  1. B4 ("Total cost"): =B1+B2.
  2. B5 ("Tickets to break even"): =B4/B3.
  3. B6 ("Verdict"): =IF(B5<=40, "GO", "RISKY").
Target: cost 240, break-even 30 tickets, verdict GO.

Now raise the room to 250 and watch the verdict flip. The sheet is not deciding for you. It is showing you exactly where the line sits, so you can argue with the inputs instead of with your gut. Want the show to work? Now you know your levers: a cheaper room, a higher ticket, or a bigger venue.

SCENARIO 7

Is the cheap one actually cheaper?

Cost per year of owning.

A $60 audio interface that dies in 2 years, or a $180 one that runs for 8? The sticker answers "what leaves my pocket today." Cost per year answers "what does owning this really cost." Different question, sometimes a different winner.

  1. B3: =B1/B2.
  2. C3: =C1/C2.
Target: budget one 30 per year, sturdy one 22.50 per year.

The trick generalizes: cost per use, cost per gig, cost per student, cost per mile. Whenever two options refuse to compare, divide both by the thing you actually care about and they will.

SANDBOX

Ask your own question.

A bigger grid, no goals, no checker. Pick a question you actually have and rough it out. Some starters:

  • What would moving to a cheaper place really save in a year?
  • What does my coffee habit cost per semester?
  • How many lessons do I need to teach before the new amp is paid off?
  • Which of two summer plans leaves me with more in September?

Shape to aim for: labels down the left, inputs you can change, formulas that point at them, and if you want a straight answer, an IF verdict at the bottom.

EXAMPLE SHEETS

Real sheets to open and dissect.

Reading other people's spreadsheets is how you get good, the same way listening is how you learn to play. Everything below is free, lives in (or copies into) Google Sheets, and asks for no signup. Pick your level, open one, and go hunting for the three jobs: labels, inputs, formulas.

Simple

Google's own Monthly Budget template

Open sheets.google.com, click Template gallery at the top, choose Monthly budget. It is the cleanest beginner sheet there is: a summary page fed by a transactions page.

Your mission: click the big total on the summary tab and read its formula. Where does the number actually come from?

Simple

Spreadsheet Class template library

spreadsheetclass.com/templates has two dozen free small templates: to-do lists, expense trackers, schedules, a gradebook. Small enough to understand completely.

Your mission: copy one, then break it on purpose and fix it. That is a full lesson in ten minutes.

Intermediate

The Measure of a Plan: Budget Tracking Tool

themeasureofaplan.com/budget-tracking-tool is a genuinely free tracker with a dashboard: you enter transactions, and charts of income, spending, and savings build themselves.

Your mission: trace one dashboard chart back to the cells it reads. It is the budget scenario from this page, grown up.

Advanced

Aspire Budgeting

aspirebudget.com is a full zero-based budgeting system in one Google Sheet: every dollar gets assigned a job, and a dashboard tracks category balances and monthly activity.

Your mission: figure out why it is split across tabs, and what each tab is allowed to know about the others. That is spreadsheet architecture.

Advanced

Vertex42: Loan Amortization Schedule

vertex42.com offers a free Google Sheets loan amortizer: payment size, interest versus principal, and what extra payments actually save.

Your mission: find the PMT function inside, then change one input and explain to yourself why the payoff date moved. Student loans stop being abstract right about here.

Not sure which tier is yours? The two-minute level quiz will point you at a starting line. Prefer to watch before you touch? Three vetted short videos live in Spreadsheet Explorations.
